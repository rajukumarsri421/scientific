What is Carbon Sequestration?

Carbon sequestration is the process of capturing and storing carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere to reduce its concentration and mitigate the impacts of climate change. It occurs in two main forms: natural and artificial. Natural carbon sequestration happens through processes such as photosynthesis, where plants, trees, and soils absorb CO₂. Oceans also play a significant role by absorbing large amounts of carbon from the atmosphere. Artificial carbon sequestration, on the other hand, involves technologies like carbon capture and storage (CCS), where CO₂ emissions from industrial sources and power plants are captured before they can be released into the atmosphere. The captured CO₂ is then transported and stored underground in geological formations like depleted oil and gas fields or deep saline aquifers. Both natural and artificial methods of carbon sequestration help reduce atmospheric CO₂ levels, which is crucial for slowing global warming and its associated environmental impacts.

Impact on Concrete Strength

Through carbon sequestration, concrete not only becomes more eco-friendly, but also gains enhanced strength by carbonation of calcium compounds, making it stronger and more durable.

Challenges

While promising, carbon sequestration technologies face challenges such as high initial costs, energy consumption, and long-term storage uncertainties. Overcoming these hurdles is crucial for global scale implementation.
